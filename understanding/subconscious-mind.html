Brain

  • Think of your brain as the command center for everything you do—it’s the boss. It controls your thoughts, feelings, actions, and even stuff you don’t think about, like breathing or your heartbeat. Now, when we talk about the mind, we’re not just talking about the brain as a physical organ; we’re talking about the way the brain processes everything.
  • Here’s where it gets interesting: your mind has two parts that work together—the conscious mind and the subconscious mind.

What’s the Conscious Mind?

  • The conscious mind is the part you’re actively aware of. It’s what you use when you’re focusing on something, like reading this explanation or deciding what to eat for dinner. Think of it like the driver of a car—it’s in charge of immediate decisions and actions.
  • But here’s the thing: your conscious mind can only handle so much at once. It’s like a small spotlight—it shines on what’s in front of you, but it can’t light up everything. That’s where the subconscious mind comes in.

What’s the Subconscious Mind?

  • The subconscious mind is like the massive engine running in the background. It’s not something you’re usually aware of, but it’s doing most of the heavy lifting. It handles all the automatic processes in your body and stores everything you’ve ever experienced—your memories, habits, beliefs, and even the stuff you don’t realize you’re holding onto.
  • Imagine your subconscious as a giant warehouse filled with boxes. Each box contains pieces of your life—happy moments, lessons you’ve learned, habits you’ve formed, and even things like how to tie your shoes or ride a bike. You don’t think about those things every day, but they’re stored there, ready to be used whenever needed.

How Does the Subconscious Work?

  • Your subconscious is always working, even when you’re asleep. It’s like the behind-the-scenes crew of a movie—setting up the scenes, managing the props, and making sure everything runs smoothly so the conscious mind (the lead actor) can do its job.
  • Here’s how it works:
    • Habits: Once you’ve done something repeatedly, your subconscious takes over. For example, when you learned to brush your teeth, you had to think about each step. Now, it’s automatic—you don’t even have to think about it.
    • Memories: It stores everything, even stuff you’ve forgotten. Ever smelled something that suddenly reminded you of a childhood memory? That’s your subconscious pulling out an old file.
    • Beliefs: Over time, it absorbs beliefs about yourself and the world. Some of these are helpful (“I can do hard things”), and some might hold you back (“I’m not good at math”).

Why Is It So Powerful?

  • Here’s the crazy part: the subconscious mind controls about 90% of what you do. That means most of your decisions, actions, and even feelings are influenced by this part of your mind. The conscious mind might think it’s in charge, but it’s like the tip of an iceberg—what’s underneath (the subconscious) is much bigger and more powerful.
  • For example:
    • When you meet someone new, your subconscious immediately processes their body language, tone of voice, and even tiny details you don’t consciously notice. That’s why you might get a “gut feeling” about someone—it’s your subconscious sending you a signal based on what it’s picked up.

How Can It Help or Hurt You?

  • The subconscious can be your best friend or your biggest obstacle, depending on what’s stored in it.
    • If it’s full of positive beliefs: You’ll feel confident, make better decisions, and move toward your goals.
    • If it’s full of negative beliefs or clutter: It might hold you back, make you doubt yourself, or leave you feeling stuck.
  • That’s why reflecting on your experiences is so important. When you take time to think about what you’ve learned, enjoyed, or struggled with, you’re essentially organizing your subconscious. It’s like cleaning out that big warehouse, labeling the boxes, and throwing away the junk.

An Everyday Example

  • Think about learning to drive. At first, you’re using your conscious mind for everything—checking mirrors, pressing pedals, remembering road signs. It feels overwhelming! But after a while, it becomes second nature. Your subconscious takes over, so now you can drive while having a conversation or planning your day.
  • The same process happens with all your habits, beliefs, and reactions. Once your subconscious gets involved, things become automatic—but only if you’ve trained it well.

In Simple Terms

  • The subconscious mind is like your brain’s autopilot. It keeps you running, stores your life’s experiences, and shapes your habits and beliefs. While it’s not something you control directly, you can influence it by being mindful of what you focus on and reflecting on your experiences.

Your subconscious is the quiet powerhouse of your mind, shaping your actions, habits, and beliefs, even when you don’t realize it.
